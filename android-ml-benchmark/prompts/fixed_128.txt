The mitochondrion is a double-membrane-bound organelle found in most eukaryotic cells. It generates most of the cell's supply of adenosine triphosphate, used as a source of chemical energy. Mitochondria are commonly between 0.75 and 3 micrometers in diameter but vary considerably in size and structure. Unless specifically stained, they are not visible under a light microscope. In addition to supplying cellular energy, mitochondria are involved in other tasks such as signaling, cellular differentiation, and cell death, as well as maintaining control of the cell cycle and cell growth. Mitochondrial biogenesis is in turn temporally coordinated with these cellular processes. Explain, step by step, how oxidative phosphorylation produces ATP inside this organelle.
